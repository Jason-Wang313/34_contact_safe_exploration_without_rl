\noindent\textbf{Submission-hardening version: v3 final full-scale.}
This revision replaces the compact v2 mechanism note with a full-scale
synthetic study of contact-safe exploration. The suite represents
123,863,040 candidate contact-probe decisions across 10 contact-field
families, 12 operating regimes, 14 policies, 96 deterministic seeds, 192
contact cells per seed, and 4 candidate probe forces per cell.

\begin{abstract}
Robots often need to explore by touching the world, but contact exploration is
where unsafe mistakes become scratches, jams, force-limit violations, tool
damage, and unrecoverable object motion. Safe reinforcement learning treats
this as constrained policy learning. This paper studies a narrower mechanism:
contact-safe exploration without RL. The robot grows a certified frontier of
allowed probes using local force/displacement evidence and expands only when a
safety filter permits the next contact. The v3 evaluation covers
123,863,040 represented candidate probe decisions. Random force probing covers
139.7 cells on average but incurs 41.30 violations per seed. Uniform low-force
probing causes almost no violations but covers only 55.0 cells. A fixed-margin
certificate improves utility but remains brittle under discontinuities.
Adaptive certificates reach 289.6 utility; MPC-style safety filtering reaches
341.2; oracle contact-limit knowledge reaches 417.8. Overconfident
certificates cover 181.5 cells but incur 98.66 violations per seed and collapse
under high-harm contact. The claim is conditional: contact certificates are
useful when their local smoothness, sensing, discontinuity, and harm
assumptions are calibrated, and harmful when they are treated as permission to
probe aggressively.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Exploration in robotics is not just information gathering. A contact probe can
scratch a surface, jam a part, exceed a force limit, slip along a friction
cliff, snag a cable, dent a thin shell, or move a workpiece into a state from
which the task cannot recover. In that setting, unsafe samples are not merely
bad data points. They are physical events.

Safe reinforcement learning is one response to this problem. It asks how to
learn a policy while respecting constraints, reducing unsafe trials, or
filtering actions. That framing is powerful, but it is not always the first
abstraction a robot needs. Before a robot learns which contact policy produces
the best long-run reward, it may need to answer a simpler question: which touch
is certified safe to attempt next?

This paper studies that question as a mechanism. We call the mechanism
\emph{certificate-guided contact exploration}. The robot maintains a frontier
of contact cells and candidate probe forces. It updates local estimates of
safe force/displacement limits after successful probes. It expands only when a
certificate says that the next probe lies below a conservative bound. If the
certificate is weak, the robot probes lower, abstains, or asks for
calibration. This is safe set growth, not reward learning.

The compact v2 version of the paper showed the core tradeoff on a toy contact
field. Random force exploration covered many cells but caused many violations.
Conservative probing caused almost no violations but did not explore far.
Fixed certificate expansion gave the best smooth-field tradeoff, but a
discontinuous-contact stress exposed its weakness: brittle low-limit pockets
can make a local smoothness certificate overconfident. The v2 result was useful
but far too small for a final manuscript.

The v3 paper scales the mechanism test. The suite represents 123,863,040
candidate contact-probe decisions. It covers smooth elastic contact, brittle
low-limit pockets, anisotropic brush contact, friction cliffs, thin shells,
foam deformation, cable snags, peg-in-hole edges, soft-tissue proxies, and a
calibration-control surface. It includes smooth nominal operation, dense
discontinuities, sparse catastrophic hazards, force-sensor noise, sensor-bias
drift, delayed stop, anisotropic limits, friction shift, deformable memory,
high frontier value, high harm weight, and a free-safe-control regime.

The policy set is intentionally broad. It includes random force probing,
uniform low-force probing, frontier low-force probing, fixed-margin
certificates, adaptive-margin certificates, Lipschitz-safe expansion,
interval-reachability filtering, CBF-style filtering, MPC-style filtering,
safe active-learning UCB, tactile-gradient probing, discontinuity detection,
oracle contact-limit knowledge, and an overconfident certificate negative
control. The central result is not that one certificate is universally best.
It is that contact-safe exploration must explicitly trade coverage, frontier
value, violation severity, false-safe expansion, false blocking, and harm.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, the paper formalizes contact-safe exploration as guarded frontier growth
over candidate probes rather than as reward-policy learning. Second, it
separates several mechanisms that are often conflated: fixed margin,
Lipschitz-bound safety, interval reachability, barrier-style filtering,
short-horizon planning, active uncertainty, tactile gradients, discontinuity
detection, and oracle limit maps. Third, it provides a RAM-light standard
library synthetic suite representing 123,863,040 candidate decisions while
storing only seed summaries, aggregates, figures, and one trace. Fourth, it
states the negative boundary: an overconfident certificate is worse than
conservatism when contact harm is high or local smoothness fails.

\section{Related Work Boundary}

The hostile neighborhood is large. Safe RL studies exploration under
constraints and is the obvious comparator. Control barrier functions and
safety filters provide formal action-level guards. Model predictive control
plans short-horizon behavior under constraints. Safe active learning and safe
Bayesian optimization choose informative samples while preserving a safe set.
Tactile exploration and contact-rich manipulation study probing, sliding,
pushing, insertion, force control, and manipulation through contact. The paper
does not claim that these areas ignore safety.

The claim is narrower. In many contact-rich tasks, the robot is not yet trying
to maximize a learned reward. It is trying to discover which contacts are
allowed. That discovery problem has a physical frontier: cells, poses, surface
patches, tool configurations, and candidate forces. The frontier grows only
when local evidence supports the next probe. This is close to safe active
learning, but the state being expanded is contact permission rather than a
generic input domain. It is close to barrier filtering, but the barrier is
being calibrated by probing rather than assumed known. It is close to tactile
exploration, but the first metric is harm-aware safe expansion rather than
classification accuracy or information gain alone.

This distinction matters because reward learning can hide the most important
engineering decision. If the robot scratches the part during exploration, a
later reward update does not undo the damage. If a low-force probe reveals a
safe local neighborhood, the robot may expand without needing a policy-gradient
loop. If a discontinuity detector fires, the correct action may be to stop
expansion, not to gather another high-value sample. The mechanism therefore
turns contact exploration into a sequence of certification decisions.

\section{Problem Setup}

Let $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ denote a candidate contact cell and let $u \in
\mathcal{U}$ denote a candidate probe force, displacement, or guarded contact
command. Each cell has an unknown safe limit $\ell(q)$ and an unknown harm
scale $h(q)$. A probe is safe if $u \leq \ell(q)$ and unsafe otherwise. A
probe can also be uninformative if it is so low that it does not reveal useful
contact geometry.

The robot maintains an estimate $\hat{\ell}(q)$ and uncertainty radius
$r(q)$. A generic certificate permits the probe when
\[
  u \leq \hat{\ell}(q) - r(q) - m(q),
\]
where $m(q)$ is an additional margin determined by policy, history, and harm.
The certificate can be fixed, adaptive, Lipschitz-based, interval-based,
barrier-style, or short-horizon. If no candidate force satisfies the guard, the
robot abstains or chooses a lower probe.

The evaluation records both kinds of error. A false-safe expansion occurs when
the policy permits a probe that violates the true limit. A false-blocked safe
probe occurs when the policy refuses a probe that could have safely produced
useful information. Contact-safe exploration is valuable only if it reduces
false-safe expansions without blocking so much that the robot never learns the
frontier.

\section{Policy Families}

\paragraph{Random force.}
The random baseline samples contact forces without a certificate. It is useful
because it reveals how much coverage can be bought by accepting unsafe contact.
It is not a safe policy.

\paragraph{Uniform low force.}
The conservative baseline probes every cell with a low force. It is nearly
safe, but it often under-covers the frontier because very low probes do not
produce enough contact information.

\paragraph{Frontier low force.}
This baseline moves spatially along the frontier but still uses only low
forces. It separates spatial expansion from force escalation.

\paragraph{Fixed-margin certificate.}
This is the v2 mechanism. It uses a local estimate and a fixed margin. It is
simple and interpretable, but brittle when contact limits are discontinuous.

\paragraph{Adaptive-margin certificate.}
This policy increases its margin after surprise violations and relaxes the
margin after stable regions. It is still simple, but it does not assume that
one margin is correct everywhere.

\paragraph{Lipschitz-safe expansion.}
This policy assumes a local bound on how quickly safe limits can change. It is
strong when the bound is calibrated and weak when brittle pockets violate the
assumption.

\paragraph{Interval reachability filter.}
This policy maintains lower and upper safe-limit intervals. It allows probes
that are safe under the lower bound and blocks probes where the interval is too
wide.

\paragraph{CBF-style safety filter.}
The barrier-style policy treats probe choice as a filtered action: an
aggressive candidate is clipped or rejected if the guard margin is violated.
It is a stylized baseline, not a complete CBF controller.

\paragraph{MPC safety filter.}
The short-horizon policy evaluates future frontier value against expected
violation risk. It can be more aggressive than a static filter when the future
value is high and more conservative after risk signals.

\paragraph{Safe active-learning UCB.}
This policy probes uncertain cells only when the safe bound remains favorable.
It tests whether uncertainty-aware exploration helps without a reward-learning
loop.

\paragraph{Tactile gradient probe.}
This policy follows local gradients in measured limits. It is useful when
contact changes smoothly, but can be misled by cliffs and discontinuities.

\paragraph{Discontinuity detector.}
This policy pauses expansion after abrupt residual changes. It sacrifices some
coverage to avoid high-harm false-safe expansions.

\paragraph{Oracle limit map.}
The oracle knows the true local safe limit. It is an upper bound, not a
deployable controller.

\paragraph{Overconfident certificate.}
The negative control uses an unsafe margin. It shows that certificates are not
magic. If the certificate is overconfident, the policy may look productive
until violation harm is counted.

The trace matters because aggregate means can hide the control logic. A policy

episode to inspect.

\section{Oracle Gap}

Oracle limit maps reach 417.8 aggregate utility, 182.9 covered cells, and zero
violations. MPC-style safety filtering reaches 341.2 utility, leaving 76.6
regret to oracle. Adaptive certificates leave 128.2 regret. The oracle gap is
not a failure of the paper. It is a measure of how much value remains in better
contact-limit calibration, better discontinuity detection, better force
sensing, and better short-horizon planning.

The gap also prevents overclaiming. If a paper only compared fixed
certificates to random and low-force baselines, it could look complete. The
oracle says it is not complete. Accurate contact-limit knowledge would be much
more valuable than any hand-written margin. A deployable system should
therefore treat the certificate as a calibrated safety object, not as a
constant chosen once for all surfaces.

\section{What Contact-Safe Exploration Is Not}

Contact-safe exploration without RL is not a rejection of RL. It is a claim
about the ordering of decisions. A robot may eventually learn a reward policy,
but it still needs a safe contact frontier before it can gather contact data
without damage.

It is not a claim that certificates are automatically safe. The negative
control shows the opposite. Overconfident certificates can be worse than random
probing because they give false authority to unsafe expansion.

It is not a claim that low-force probing is obsolete. Uniform low force is a
serious baseline. In high-harm contact, a conservative probe can be the right
default until calibration improves.

It is not a claim that synthetic utility numbers transfer directly to a robot.
The suite is a mechanism study. Hardware validation must measure force limits,
harm, compliance, sensing error, delays, and operator overrides.

\section{Hardware Calibration Protocol}

A hardware version of this paper should begin with a transition from synthetic
cells to measured contact cells. For each cell or contact pose, the robot
should record candidate probe force, displacement, velocity, normal direction,
tangential direction, tool state, object state, measured force, slip, recovery
state, and damage proxy. The safe limit should not be a single global number.
It should be indexed by geometry, tool, surface, and direction.

The calibration run should include repeated low-force probes, controlled force
ramps, direction changes, and stop-latency measurements. Stop latency is
important because a controller that detects violation at time $t$ may still
move after detection. That overshoot changes the real safety certificate. A
delayed-stop regime is therefore included in the suite.

The report should separate three quantities. The first is direct limit
violation: force or displacement exceeded a threshold. The second is harm:
visible damage, deformation, slip, jam, or unrecoverable object motion. The
third is information value: whether the probe improved the contact frontier.
Mixing these quantities into one reward hides the safety argument.

\section{Deployment Protocol}

A deployable contact-safe explorer should expose its decision reason. For each
candidate probe, it should log the predicted limit, uncertainty radius, margin,
candidate force, selected force, blocked alternatives, and whether the decision
came from a fixed margin, adaptive margin, interval bound, barrier filter,
short-horizon filter, or discontinuity detector.

The system should also log abstentions. Abstention is not failure if the
certificate is weak. It is a safety decision. However, excessive abstention can
make the robot useless. The false-blocked metric is included to catch that
failure.

When a violation occurs, the robot should lower the local safe estimate,
increase the nearby margin, mark the cell as a discontinuity candidate, and
avoid treating neighboring successes as proof that the pocket is safe. A
human operator should be able to inspect the episode and decide whether the
region is permanently unsafe, requires a different tool, or needs a new
calibration pass.

\section{Limitations}

The suite is synthetic. It uses stylized contact-field families, expected
rollouts, and hand-designed policy abstractions. It does not include a
high-fidelity contact simulator, deformable-body physics, real tactile
signals, tool wear, perception error, or human reset cost. It is a mechanism
test, not a deployment result.

The policy implementations are stylized. The CBF-style filter is not a full
control barrier implementation. The MPC-style filter is a compact short-horizon
surrogate. The safe active-learning policy is not a full Gaussian-process
implementation. These are mechanism baselines, not optimized controllers.

The utility function is synthetic. It is useful because it separates coverage,
frontier value, violation severity, high-harm contact, false-safe expansion,
and false blocking. It should not be read as seconds, dollars, joules, or real
damage probability.

The strongest remaining weakness is hardware calibration. A real paper must
show that measured contact limits are smooth enough, or that discontinuity
detectors catch the cases where they are not. Without that calibration, a
certificate is only a hypothesis.

\section{Conclusion}

Contact-safe exploration without RL reframes robot probing as certified safe
frontier growth. The full-scale evidence supports a bounded claim. Fixed
certificates are better than blind force probing and low-force conservatism in
many smooth settings, but they are not enough. The useful controllers combine
calibrated safety bounds, adaptive margins, short-horizon value, uncertainty,
and discontinuity detection. The dangerous controller is the overconfident
certificate: it covers many cells, but pays for that coverage with unsafe
contact. For contact-rich robotics, the first exploration question is not
which action has the best reward. It is which action is certified safe to try.

\section{Appendix: Contact-Field Family Details}

The smooth elastic surface family is the friendly case. Safe limits vary
slowly, sensor noise is low, and local evidence transfers to neighboring
cells. Fixed certificates are expected to work here.

Brittle low-limit pockets are the hostile case for local smoothness. A cell can
look like its neighbors but have a much lower force limit. This family attacks
the assumption that nearby contacts are similar.

Anisotropic brush contact changes safe force by direction. A normal probe may
be safe while a tangential probe is unsafe. This family attacks scalar force
certificates.

Friction cliffs separate normal contact from slip. A probe can respect the
normal force limit while causing tangential motion. This family requires a
certificate that represents more than one physical mode.

Thin shell contact has high information value but low harm tolerance. It
punishes overconfident expansion because a small violation can matter.

Foam deformation has memory. A first probe can change later limits. This
attacks stationarity and requires policies to update after repeated contact.

Cable snag fields contain sparse high-harm hazards. Average smoothness is not
enough because a rare snag dominates the safety case.

Peg-in-hole edge contact places useful information near unsafe edges. It
rewards short-horizon planning because the frontier is valuable, but punishes
blind force increases.

Soft-tissue proxy is the high-harm compliant case. It rewards gentle probing
and calibrated force sensing.

Calibration-control surfaces are deliberately forgiving. They test whether the
method recognizes when aggressive probing is acceptable.

\section{Appendix: Regime Semantics}

The smooth nominal regime checks whether the certificate can exploit an easy
field. Dense discontinuities increase the frequency of brittle pockets.
Sparse catastrophic hazards make rare violations expensive. Force sensor noise
adds random measurement error. Sensor-bias drift adds systematic optimism or
pessimism. Delayed stop adds overshoot after a violation signal. Anisotropic
limits and friction shift attack scalar certificates. Deformable memory changes
limits after earlier probes. Frontier value high rewards exploration near the
unknown boundary. High harm weight tests safety priority. Free-safe control
checks whether conservatism disappears when contact is safe.

\section{Appendix: Metric Definitions}

Coverage counts cells where the selected probe is informative enough to expand
the known frontier. Frontier coverage gives extra weight to cells near the
unknown boundary. Useful information gain records partial information even when
a probe is safe but not strong enough for full coverage. Violations count
unsafe probes. Violation severity accumulates how far the effective probe
exceeds the limit. High-harm violations count unsafe probes in high-harm
regions. False-safe expansions are violations that were permitted by the
policy. False-blocked safe probes are useful probes that were refused.

Cumulative utility rewards coverage and information, then subtracts violation
harm and false-blocking cost. Harm-weighted utility increases the penalty for
unsafe contact. Regret to oracle compares each policy to the true limit map on
the same family, regime, and seed.

\section{Appendix: Why Fixed Margins Fail}

A fixed margin assumes that one buffer covers local smoothness, sensing error,
delay, and harm. This is rarely true. If the margin is small, the policy
expands quickly but violates brittle pockets. If the margin is large, the
policy blocks too much useful probing. A fixed margin can look good on average
because it balances these errors, but the balance changes across surfaces.

The v3 suite exposes that weakness. Fixed certificates reach 208.4 aggregate
utility, better than low-force probing, but far below adaptive certificates at
289.6 and MPC-style filtering at 341.2. The difference is not only more
aggression. It is conditional aggression: raise margins after surprise, lower
them after stable evidence, and stop expansion near discontinuities.

\section{Appendix: Discontinuity Detection}

The discontinuity detector is intentionally conservative. It watches for abrupt
residuals between predicted and observed contact limits. When the residual is
large, it pauses expansion or drops to low-force probing. This reduces
coverage, but it avoids treating local success as proof that the next cell is
safe.

In aggregate the detector reaches 120.0 covered cells and 268.4 utility. It is
not the global utility winner, but in high-harm controls it becomes more
attractive because zero violations can matter more than extra coverage. This is
the kind of policy a hardware system might use near expensive parts or human
tissue.

\section{Appendix: Free-Safe Control}

The free-safe-control regime is a guard against overclaiming. When violations
are impossible, random or aggressive exploration should be strong. In this
regime random force reaches 342.9 utility, overconfident certificates reach
416.7, and oracle reaches 418.5. That is not a contradiction. It is exactly the
boundary. If contact is safe, conservatism is unnecessary. The paper only
argues for certificates when unsafe contact has real cost.

\section{Appendix: High-Harm Interpretation}

High-harm contact changes the ordering. Random force becomes negative utility.
Overconfident certificates become much worse, reaching -604.6 in the high-harm
stress. Fixed certificates become weak because their violations are expensive.
Adaptive certificates and discontinuity detection are safer because they change
behavior after evidence of risk.

This matters for robotics review. A policy can look good on coverage and still
be unacceptable if its violations are rare but severe. The paper therefore
reports high-harm violations separately rather than hiding them in average
return.

\section{Appendix: Hardware Measurement Checklist}

A hardware contact-safe explorer should report: robot and tool, object
material, surface preparation, contact normal, tangential direction, force
sensor calibration, displacement sensor calibration, controller rate, stop
latency, candidate force grid, reset policy, visible damage labels, slip
labels, deformation labels, and operator overrides.

It should also report failed probes. Omitting failed probes makes the
certificate look safer than it is. A violation log is not embarrassing; it is
the evidence needed to calibrate the next margin.

\section{Appendix: Minimal Worked Example}

Consider three neighboring cells with true limits 0.58, 0.57, and 0.18. A
fixed certificate that observes the first two cells may predict the third is
safe at 0.42. That probe violates the true limit. A discontinuity detector sees
the large residual and raises the margin. An interval filter refuses the 0.42
candidate until the lower bound improves. An oracle chooses a safe low probe.

The example shows why local success is not enough. The certificate needs an
assumption about how limits change, and that assumption can be wrong.

\section{Appendix: Operator Interface}

The interface should show the candidate probe, predicted safe limit,
uncertainty, margin, selected force, blocked force, and reason. A useful reason
is not ``policy chose action 2.'' It is ``0.42 N blocked because lower bound is
0.31 N after a discontinuity residual'' or ``0.28 N allowed because interval
lower bound remains above candidate force.'' These explanations let a human
decide whether to recalibrate, override, or change tools.

transition from low-force probes to certified frontier expansion improved
coverage while reducing damage in real contact tasks.

\section{Appendix: Policy-By-Policy Failure Analysis}

Random force fails by treating contact as if samples were free. It has high
coverage, 139.7 cells, but 41.30 violations per seed. The important point is
not that random exploration is irrational. In free-safe-control settings it is
quite strong. The failure is that random force has no way to know when a cell
belongs to a brittle pocket, a thin shell, or a high-harm region.

Uniform low force fails by under-exploring. It has only 0.08 violations per
seed, which is excellent, but it covers 55.0 cells. In contact tasks where the
goal is only to confirm a few safe touches, this may be enough. In manipulation
tasks where the robot needs a frontier of possible contacts, it is too
restrictive. Its failure is false blocking, not false safety.

Frontier low force improves spatial coverage without escalating force. It
reaches 85.1 covered cells, much better than uniform low force. It remains
limited because many useful contacts require a probe stronger than the low
candidate. The policy is a useful diagnostic because it asks whether spatial
expansion alone solves the problem. In the v3 suite, it does not.

Fixed-margin certificates fail when one margin is asked to solve too many
problems. The margin must cover sensing error, local roughness, delayed stop,
deformation, anisotropy, and discontinuity. A single number cannot be right in
all regimes. Fixed certificates are better than low-force probing on many
families, but their 19.55 violations per seed show that the margin is too
optimistic in hostile contact.

Adaptive-margin certificates fail more gracefully. They raise their margin
after surprise and relax after stable regions. Their aggregate utility, 289.6,
shows that this simple adaptation removes much of the fixed-margin failure. The
remaining weakness is that adaptation is reactive: the first discontinuity may
still be discovered by violating it.

Lipschitz-safe expansion fails when the Lipschitz bound is false. If contact
limits change smoothly, the policy is principled. If a brittle pocket is
embedded in a smooth-looking surface, the bound is a false comfort. This is
why the suite includes both smooth elastic and brittle-pocket families.

Interval reachability fails by widening intervals too slowly or too quickly. If
the lower bound is too optimistic, the policy violates. If it is too
pessimistic, the policy blocks safe probes. Its low violation rate, 1.07 per
seed, is strong, but it gives up coverage relative to MPC-style filtering.

CBF-style filtering fails when the barrier is calibrated on the wrong model.
The barrier can clip unsafe candidates, but if the contact-limit estimate is
wrong, the filter inherits that error. This paper therefore treats it as a
baseline family, not as a magic safety proof.

MPC-style filtering fails when the short-horizon model is wrong. It has the
best non-oracle aggregate utility, 341.2, because it values future frontier
coverage. But the same horizon can become harmful if it overestimates the
value of reaching a risky frontier. A hardware version should report horizon
sensitivity.

Safe active-learning UCB fails when uncertainty is misread as permission. It
is strong in the suite, 327.1 utility, because it probes uncertain cells only
when the safe bound remains favorable. If the upper confidence structure is
calibrated incorrectly, it can become either timid or unsafe.

Tactile-gradient probing fails at cliffs. Gradients are useful on smooth
surfaces, but a friction cliff or brittle pocket can make the next step very
different from the previous one. Its 12.30 violations per seed show that
gradients alone are not sufficient.

Discontinuity detection fails by being cautious. It gives up coverage, reaching
120.0 cells, but it avoids violations in the expected rollout. This is a good
trade in high-harm contact and a poor trade in free-safe contact. The value of
the detector is therefore domain dependent.

Oracle limit maps fail only as a deployment claim. They are not available in
practice. Their role is to show what calibrated contact knowledge would buy:
417.8 utility and zero violations. The oracle is a ceiling, not a method.

Overconfident certificates fail in the most dangerous way. They look like a
sophisticated safety method, but their margin is unsafe. They cover 181.5
cells, nearly oracle coverage, while causing 98.66 violations per seed. This
is why the paper treats false authority as worse than no certificate.

\section{Appendix: Regime-By-Regime Reading Guide}

Smooth nominal contact tests whether certificates can use friendly physics.
The answer is yes: fixed certificates improve over conservative probing, and
adaptive and MPC-style filters improve further. This regime verifies the
positive mechanism.

Dense discontinuities test the core negative boundary. The fixed certificate
still functions, but it loses utility because many local smoothness assumptions
are false. Adaptive margins and discontinuity detection are the intended
responses.

Sparse catastrophic hazards test whether average-case coverage hides rare
failures. A policy can perform well on most cells and still be unacceptable if
one snag or thin-shell puncture is costly. Reporting high-harm violations is
therefore mandatory.

Force sensor noise tests stochastic calibration. A certificate that ignores
noise is overconfident. Interval and UCB-style policies explicitly represent
uncertainty and therefore retain low violation rates.

Sensor-bias drift tests systematic calibration. Random noise can be averaged;
bias cannot. A biased sensor may make every contact look safer than it is. The
appropriate response is not only larger margins, but periodic recalibration and
cross-checking against low-force probes.

Delayed stop tests controller dynamics. A probe selected below the limit can
still exceed the limit if the robot overshoots after detection. Hardware papers
often report force thresholds but omit stop latency. That omission is unsafe.

Anisotropic limits test scalar certificates. A normal force can be safe while
tangential motion is unsafe. A deployed certificate should be indexed by
direction and contact mode, not only by a scalar force value.

Friction shift tests slip. Slip can be a violation even when normal force is
acceptable. A contact explorer needs a model of friction, tangential motion,
and surface state.

Deformable memory tests stationarity. A probe can change the object, so the
next probe does not see the same field. This regime is especially relevant for
foam, tissue, flexible packaging, and soft grippers.

Frontier value high tests whether policies can be productively bold. If a
frontier region has high value, a good policy may accept some additional risk.
The point is not zero risk; it is calibrated risk.

High harm weight tests safety priority. Here the ranking changes. Policies
that looked strong under ordinary utility become unacceptable when violation
harm dominates. This is the regime that exposes overconfident certificates.

Free-safe control tests the other boundary. If contact is safe, conservative
certificates should not dominate. Aggressive exploration is allowed to win.

\section{Appendix: Certificate Design Matrix}

A certificate has at least seven design choices. First, it chooses the contact
state: cell, pose, tool, normal direction, tangential direction, object state,
or a combination. Second, it chooses the candidate action: force,
displacement, velocity, impedance target, or guarded motion primitive. Third,
it chooses the evidence model: local average, interval, Lipschitz bound,
barrier function, short-horizon planner, tactile gradient, or learned
surrogate. Fourth, it chooses the margin. Fifth, it chooses the update rule
after success. Sixth, it chooses the update rule after violation. Seventh, it
chooses when to abstain.

The v3 suite includes policy families that occupy different cells of this
design matrix. The fixed certificate uses a local estimate and fixed margin.
The adaptive certificate changes the margin after surprise. Lipschitz-safe
expansion assumes bounded local change. Interval reachability maintains lower
and upper limits. CBF-style filtering clips candidate actions through a guard.
MPC-style filtering evaluates a short horizon. Safe active learning uses
uncertainty. The discontinuity detector changes the state of the frontier after
abrupt residuals.

This matrix is useful because it prevents the paper from overselling one
implementation. The contribution is not the exact margin in the synthetic
runner. The contribution is the mechanism structure: contact exploration
should expose a certificate object whose assumptions can be measured, attacked,
and updated.

\section{Appendix: Calibration Equations}

A simple hardware certificate can begin with repeated low-force probes. Let
$f_i$ be the maximum measured force that caused no violation at cell $i$, and
let $s_i$ be a binary safety label for candidate force $u_i$. A conservative
estimate can be written as
\[
  \hat{\ell}_i = \bar{f}_i - z_\alpha \sigma_i - \delta_{\mathrm{stop}},
\]
where $\bar{f}_i$ is the empirical safe force, $\sigma_i$ is measurement
uncertainty, $z_\alpha$ is a confidence multiplier, and
$\delta_{\mathrm{stop}}$ is stop-latency overshoot. A candidate is allowed when
\[
  u_i \leq \hat{\ell}_i - m_i .
\]

For a Lipschitz certificate, neighboring cells provide a lower bound:
\[
  \hat{\ell}_j \geq \hat{\ell}_i - L d(i,j) - z_\alpha\sigma_i -
  \delta_{\mathrm{stop}}.
\]
The bound is useful only if $L$ is calibrated. A brittle pocket falsifies it.
For an interval certificate, the robot stores
$[\ell_i^-, \ell_i^+]$ and allows only probes below $\ell_i^-$. For an
adaptive certificate, $m_i$ increases after a residual
$|\hat{\ell}_i - \ell_i|$ exceeds a threshold.

These equations are intentionally simple. They show what a reviewer should ask
for: the confidence level, sensor uncertainty, stop-latency term, local
distance metric, and residual update rule. A certificate without these
quantities is a slogan, not a safety argument.

\section{Appendix: Candidate Force Grid Sensitivity}

The v3 suite uses four candidate probe levels per cell. This is not a claim
that four levels are optimal. A finer grid can improve coverage because the
robot may find a safe force just below the limit. It can also increase runtime
and require more calibration. A coarser grid makes decisions simpler but may
force the robot to choose between uninformative low force and unsafe high
force.

The represented decision count includes candidate probes because the safety
filter must conceptually reject or allow each candidate. The runner summarizes
those candidates rather than storing every candidate-level row. A hardware
paper should report the candidate grid because it changes both safety and
coverage. A controller tested with only tiny probes is not comparable to one
tested with aggressive force options.

\section{Appendix: Force Sensor Calibration}

Force sensing is not a neutral measurement. Bias, drift, quantization, and
mount compliance can all change the certificate. If the sensor reads low, the
robot may think a dangerous probe is safe. If it reads high, the robot may
block useful probes. Both errors matter.

A calibration protocol should include zeroing before contact, known-weight
checks, direction-specific calibration, repeated contact ramps, temperature
drift checks, and post-run recalibration. The final paper should report both
random noise and systematic bias. The v3 suite includes separate force noise
and sensor-bias regimes because those errors require different remedies.

\section{Appendix: Stop Latency And Overshoot}

In contact safety, detecting a violation is not the same as preventing it. The
controller has a loop rate, the actuator has inertia, the contact has
compliance, and the command has delay. If the robot detects excessive force at
time $t$, it may still move after $t$. The overshoot should be subtracted from
the safe limit.

This is the role of $\delta_{\mathrm{stop}}$ in the calibration equation. A
robot with fast force control can use a smaller term. A robot with slow
actuators or high stiffness needs a larger term. A certificate that omits stop
latency is overconfident by construction.

\section{Appendix: Anisotropy And Friction}

A scalar force threshold is often insufficient. Brushing a surface, sliding
along an edge, inserting a peg, and pressing normal to a plane can have very
different risks. The same normal force may be safe in one direction and unsafe
in another. Friction can change suddenly with surface patch, contamination,
humidity, wear, or tool material.

The anisotropic and friction-shift regimes are included to attack the scalar
certificate. A hardware certificate should index limits by contact mode. At
minimum, it should separate normal force, tangential force, slip, and tool
orientation. A single number cannot represent all of these modes.

\section{Appendix: Deformable Memory}

Some contact fields change after the robot touches them. Foam compresses.
Tissue deforms. Packaging dents. A cable moves. A thin shell weakens. A
successful first probe can make the second probe unsafe, or an unsafe first
probe can change the geometry enough that later evidence is misleading.

Deformable memory breaks the assumption that the contact map is fixed. The
safe frontier should therefore include history. A simple approximation is to
decay confidence after repeated probing in the same region. A better hardware
system would model deformation state explicitly.

\section{Appendix: Statistical Reporting Plan}

The suite reports deterministic seed means and standard errors. A hardware
paper should report more. It should give trial counts, confidence intervals,
surface-level random effects, robot-level random effects, and paired
comparisons where each policy sees the same contact field. It should report
not only average utility but also maximum violation severity and high-harm
event counts.

For reviewer clarity, the primary comparison should be paired by object and
contact region. The robot should test low-force, fixed certificate, adaptive
certificate, safety filter, and human-approved oracle-like reference on the
same calibrated regions when possible. If destructive tests prevent paired
evaluation, the paper should explain the blocking design.

\section{Appendix: Field Log Schema}

A useful field log has one row per candidate decision. Columns should include:
episode id, robot id, tool id, object id, cell id, pose, normal direction,
tangential direction, candidate force, selected force, blocked candidates,
predicted limit, uncertainty, margin, stop-latency term, sensor bias estimate,
certificate type, decision reason, measured peak force, displacement, slip,
damage label, reset label, operator override, and post-contact frontier state.

This schema is longer than the synthetic CSV because hardware contact has more
ways to fail. It is also necessary. Without candidate-level logs, one cannot
distinguish false-safe expansion from false-blocked safe probes. Without
operator overrides, one cannot tell whether a human rejected the certificate
for a reason the model failed to represent.

\section{Appendix: Acceptance Tests}

Test one is the smooth expansion test. On a calibrated smooth surface, the
certificate should expand beyond low-force probing while keeping violations
low. If it cannot, the margin is too conservative or the evidence update is
broken.

Test two is the brittle pocket test. Insert a hidden low-limit pocket. The
policy should either detect the discontinuity before a severe violation or
raise margins after the first surprise. If it keeps expanding, it is
overconfident.

Test three is the high-harm test. Mark some cells as high harm. The policy
should reduce risky probes even if expected coverage falls. If it maximizes
coverage, it is not harm aware.

Test four is the free-safe test. Remove violation harm. The policy should not
remain unnecessarily conservative. If it still blocks aggressive probes, the
certificate has become a habit rather than a safety argument.

Test five is the sensor-bias test. Add systematic under-reporting of force.
The policy should detect inconsistency through calibration checks or widen
margins. If it trusts the biased estimate, it is unsafe.

Test six is the delayed-stop test. Increase controller overshoot. The policy
should subtract stop latency from the safe bound. If it ignores overshoot, it
will violate even when nominal probe force is below the limit.

\section{Appendix: Safety Case}

A safety case for contact-safe exploration should make four claims. First, the
candidate probe set is bounded. Second, the certificate is conservative with
respect to measured uncertainty and stop latency. Third, the update rule after
success and violation is logged and auditable. Fourth, there is a fallback
policy for weak certificates.

Each claim needs evidence. Bounded candidates need force and displacement
limits. Conservative certificates need calibration data. Update rules need logs
showing margin changes after residuals. Fallback policies need tests showing
that abstention or low-force probing actually prevents harm. Without this
evidence, the system should not be called safe.

\section{Appendix: Deployment Failure Playbook}

If violations are high and coverage is high, the policy is too aggressive or
overconfident. Increase margins, improve discontinuity detection, or reduce
candidate force levels.

If violations are low and coverage is low, the policy is too conservative.
Inspect false-blocked safe probes, reduce margins where calibration supports
it, or add low-risk frontier probes.

If violations cluster in one direction, the certificate is missing anisotropy.
Separate normal and tangential limits.

If violations cluster after repeated probing, the contact field has memory.
Add deformation state or decay confidence.

If violations occur after detection, the controller has stop-latency overshoot.
Measure and subtract it.

If a human repeatedly overrides the same decision, the model is missing an
operational variable. Add that variable to the log and certificate.

\section{Appendix: Comparison To Reward Learning}

Reward learning can still be useful after a safe frontier exists. Once the
robot knows a region of safe probes, it can learn which contact sequence is
efficient, informative, or task completing. The point of this paper is that
the frontier should not be discovered by trial-and-error reward optimization
when unsafe trials are costly.

A hybrid system is natural. Use certificates to define the allowed action set.
Use learning inside that set. When learning proposes an action outside the
certified set, route it through the safety filter or require calibration. This
division lets learning improve performance without making every unsafe contact
an acceptable exploration sample.

\section{Appendix: Comparison To Barrier Filtering}

Barrier filtering and contact certificates are compatible. A barrier filter can
enforce a constraint during motion. The contact certificate can decide whether
the candidate contact should be attempted at all. The barrier depends on a
model; the certificate may be part of how that model is discovered.

The v3 CBF-style baseline is intentionally simplified. It tests the mechanism
family, not a full theorem. A hardware paper that claims a formal barrier
should state the dynamics, the barrier function, the safe set, the controller,
and the proof obligations. This paper instead asks how the safe set is grown
when the contact limit is initially unknown.

\section{Appendix: Comparison To MPC Safety Filters}

MPC-style filtering performs well because contact exploration is sequential.
The value of a probe depends on what it enables next. A low-force probe may be
worth taking if it opens a safe frontier. A high-force probe may be blocked if
its future value does not justify risk. This is why the MPC-style baseline is
the strongest non-oracle method in aggregate.

The weakness is model dependence. A short-horizon plan can be wrong about
deformation, friction, stop latency, or discontinuity. The hardware protocol
should therefore report horizon length and sensitivity. If the horizon must be
tuned separately for every object, the method is less general than the
aggregate number suggests.

\section{Appendix: Human Supervision}

Human supervision should be treated as part of the system, not as an
afterthought. The operator should see why a probe was allowed or blocked. The
operator should be able to label a region as fragile, approve a lower probe,
forbid a tool direction, or request recalibration. Each override should be
logged with reason codes.

This interface matters because contact harm is context dependent. A force that
is safe on scrap material may be unacceptable on a customer's part. A probe
that is safe for a rigid tool may be unsafe with a sharp edge. The certificate
needs human-readable variables so that domain experts can audit it.

\section{Appendix: Release Checklist For A Hardware Artifact}

Before release, the artifact should include source code, calibration scripts,
raw contact logs, processed metrics, object descriptions, tool descriptions,
force sensor specifications, controller rate, stop-latency measurement,
candidate probe grid, damage-label protocol, and videos of representative
successes and failures. It should include failed runs. It should include the
exact policy parameters used in each condition. It should include a script that
recomputes tables from logs.

The paper should also state what was not tested. If the system was never tested
on deformable objects, say so. If it was never tested near humans, say so. If
the certificate assumes rigid surfaces, say so. Clear boundaries make the work
more useful, not less.

\section{Appendix: Synthetic Generator Semantics}

The synthetic generator is not a physics engine. It is a controlled mechanism
generator. Each family contributes a base safe limit, smooth variation,
discontinuity probability, anisotropy pressure, friction pressure,
deformability, hazard pressure, frontier value, and harm scale. Each regime
then multiplies or shifts those quantities. A seed fixes the resulting contact
field summary. Policies do not see the true limit unless they are the oracle.

The generator deliberately separates factors that real contact often entangles.
For example, a soft-tissue proxy has low base limits and high harm. A
friction-cliff surface has moderate normal limits but high tangential risk. A
cable snag field has sparse high-harm pockets. A calibration-control surface
has wide safe margins. This separation lets the suite ask which assumption a
policy needs.

The expected rollout represents the outcome of testing all candidate
family/regime/policy/seed/cell/probe combinations while storing only seed-level
summaries. It should be read like a large factorial stress test, not as a claim
about one simulated robot. This is why the paper reports the represented
candidate decision count and not a wall-clock robot episode count.

\section{Appendix: Utility-Weight Sensitivity}

The paper uses cumulative utility as a reading aid, not as the only result. If
coverage is weighted more heavily, aggressive methods improve. Random force
and overconfident certificates look better because they cover many cells. If
harm is weighted more heavily, low-force probing and discontinuity detection
improve. If false blocking is weighted more heavily, interval and CBF-style
filters must become less conservative. If frontier value is weighted more
heavily, MPC-style filtering improves because it considers short-horizon
frontier value.

This sensitivity is why the paper reports raw metrics beside utility. A reader
who disagrees with the utility weights can still inspect covered cells,
violations per seed, violation severity, high-harm violations, and regret to
oracle. The central qualitative result survives these readings: random probing
buys coverage with violations, low force buys safety with under-coverage, fixed
certificates are brittle, adaptive and short-horizon filters are stronger, and
overconfidence is unsafe.

\section{Appendix: Worked Episode On A Smooth Surface}

On a smooth elastic surface, the robot starts with a low-force probe. The
measured limit agrees with neighboring cells. A fixed certificate permits the
next medium probe because the predicted lower bound remains above the candidate
force. The probe succeeds and expands the frontier. After several successes,
an adaptive certificate relaxes its margin slightly, and an MPC-style filter
chooses a stronger probe because the next frontier cells have high value.

This is the positive case. The certificate turns local contact evidence into
permission to explore. No reward learning is needed to decide that a candidate
probe is allowed. The key assumption is that the smoothness evidence is
actually valid. When it is valid, certificates prevent unnecessary
conservatism.

\section{Appendix: Worked Episode On A Brittle Pocket}

Now consider a field where two neighboring cells have safe limits near 0.56
and the next cell has a hidden limit near 0.16. A fixed certificate predicts
that the next cell is similar to its neighbors and permits a 0.42 probe. The
probe violates. The failure is not random; it is a direct consequence of a
false smoothness assumption.

An adaptive certificate still may violate the first pocket, but it increases
the margin afterward. A discontinuity detector treats the residual as evidence
that the local field is unreliable and pauses expansion. An interval filter may
refuse the stronger candidate until its lower bound improves. The oracle
chooses a safe low probe. This episode is the reason the paper does not claim
that fixed certificates solve contact safety.

\section{Appendix: Worked Episode Under Sensor Bias}

Suppose the true safe limit is 0.35, but the force sensor is biased so that the
estimate reads 0.42. A certificate with a 0.05 margin may permit a 0.36 probe.
Nominally, 0.36 is below the biased estimate minus margin. Physically, it is
above the true limit. The certificate violates because the measurement model is
wrong.

The remedy is not only a larger fixed margin. A larger margin may block too
much safe contact elsewhere. The remedy is to report sensor bias, recalibrate,
use independent checks, and increase uncertainty in regions where residuals
suggest systematic error. This is why the v3 suite separates random sensor
noise from bias drift.

\section{Appendix: Worked Episode Under Delayed Stop}

Suppose the selected probe force is 0.32 and the true limit is 0.34. A static
certificate says the probe is safe. But if the controller overshoots by 0.05
after detecting contact, the effective force becomes 0.37 and the probe
violates. The certificate was correct about the commanded force and wrong about
the physical event.

This is a common robotics issue. Software limits are not physical limits unless
control delay, actuator dynamics, compliance, and stop latency are included.
The hardware protocol therefore requires a measured stop-latency term in the
safe bound.

\section{Appendix: Reviewer-Facing Comparison Table In Words}

Compared with safe RL, this paper removes reward-policy learning from the
first safety decision. Compared with CBFs, it asks how the contact safe set is
discovered when the model is unknown. Compared with MPC safety filters, it adds
calibration and discontinuity stress. Compared with safe active learning, it
grounds the safe set in physical contact limits. Compared with tactile
classification, it evaluates whether the probe was safe enough to attempt.
Compared with contact-rich manipulation, it isolates the exploration phase
before task execution.

These comparisons are phrased carefully because the paper should not claim
that prior work lacks safety. It should claim that contact exploration needs an
explicit artifact: a certificate that says why a candidate touch is allowed.

\section{Appendix: Practical Parameter Checklist}

For each certificate policy, report the initial margin, margin update after
success, margin update after violation, uncertainty multiplier, stop-latency
term, candidate force grid, discontinuity threshold, maximum allowed force,
minimum informative force, abstention rule, and reset rule. If using a
short-horizon filter, report the horizon length and frontier-value weights. If
using an interval filter, report the interval update. If using a barrier-style
filter, report the barrier function and model assumptions.

The checklist prevents hidden tuning. A certificate can look impressive if the
margin is chosen after seeing the test field. A real artifact should state how
parameters were chosen before evaluation and which calibration data were used.

\section{Appendix: Negative Controls}

The overconfident certificate is the main negative control. It asks what
happens when the same certificate structure is given an unsafe margin. The
answer is clear: high coverage and low safety. This negative control is
important because otherwise the paper might look like it is merely rewarding
the word ``certificate.''

The free-safe-control regime is the second negative control. It asks what
happens when safety constraints are effectively absent. In that regime,
aggressive methods should be competitive. If the certificate still wins only
by blocking probes, the method is solving the wrong problem. The v3 results
pass this sanity check: aggressive methods become strong when violations are
impossible.

\section{Appendix: External Validity Checklist}

A reviewer should ask whether the real task has costly unsafe contact. If not,
the paper's mechanism is unnecessary. The reviewer should ask whether local
contact limits are smooth enough for certificates. If not, discontinuity
detection or conservative probing may dominate. The reviewer should ask
whether the robot can measure force and stop quickly enough. If not, the
certificate is only nominal. The reviewer should ask whether the candidate
probe grid includes meaningful actions. If not, coverage comparisons are
misleading.

The reviewer should also ask whether the task rewards contact frontier
coverage. If the robot only needs one known safe contact, uniform low-force
probing may be enough. If the robot needs to manipulate, insert, slide, or
explore many contacts, certified frontier growth becomes more valuable.

\section{Appendix: Long-Form Summary Of The Evidence}

The main table shows four clusters. The first cluster is unsafe exploration:
random force and overconfident certificates. They have high coverage, but their
violations dominate in harmful regimes. The second cluster is conservative
exploration: uniform and frontier low force. These policies are safe but leave
frontier value unclaimed. The third cluster is certificate filtering: fixed,
adaptive, Lipschitz, interval, CBF-style, MPC-style, UCB, tactile-gradient, and
discontinuity policies. These policies trade coverage and harm with different
assumptions. The fourth cluster is oracle limit knowledge, which sets the
ceiling.

The important comparison is not random versus oracle. The important comparison
is fixed certificate versus adaptive and safety-filtered certificates. Fixed
certificates are already a meaningful mechanism, but their failure modes are
predictable. Adaptive, interval, and horizon-based filters are stronger because
they expose more of the contact safety structure. This is the strongest form of
the paper's claim.

\section{Appendix: Contact-Risk Taxonomy}

Contact risk has several forms that should not be collapsed into one violation
counter. The first is force-limit risk: the robot presses harder than the
object or tool can tolerate. The second is displacement risk: the robot moves
too far after contact and changes the object state. The third is slip risk:
normal force remains acceptable but tangential motion damages the task. The
fourth is snag risk: the tool catches on a cable, lip, fabric, or edge. The
fifth is deformation risk: the contact is safe initially but changes the future
contact field. The sixth is contamination risk: the probe leaves residue or
changes surface condition. The seventh is human-context risk: the same
physical force is acceptable in one workspace and unacceptable near a person,
medical object, or customer part.

The v3 families cover several of these risks, but not all. Friction cliffs
represent slip. Cable snag fields represent snag. Foam and soft-tissue proxies
represent deformation and high harm. Thin shells represent force and
displacement fragility. A hardware paper should state which risk classes are
inside its certificate and which are outside. If the certificate only covers
normal force, it should not be described as contact safe in general.

\section{Appendix: Calibration Audit Questions}

Before trusting a contact certificate, ask how the safe limit was measured. Was
it measured on the same object, with the same tool, at the same approach speed,
in the same direction, and after the same number of prior probes? Was sensor
bias checked before and after the run? Was stop latency measured under contact,
or only in free space? Were failed probes included in calibration? Were
operator overrides recorded? Was damage labeled by a human, an automatic
threshold, or both?

The second set of questions concerns transfer. Does a safe limit measured on
one cell transfer to a neighbor? What distance metric defines neighbor? Does
the metric include surface curvature, material, tool orientation, and contact
mode? What residual is large enough to declare a discontinuity? What happens
after a discontinuity is declared? Does the policy lower force, abstain, ask a
human, or switch tools?

The third set concerns reporting. Are the candidate forces known before
evaluation? Are margins chosen before seeing test surfaces? Are high-harm
events reported separately? Is the oracle used only as an upper bound? Are
negative controls included? These questions are mundane, but they decide
whether a certificate is an auditable safety object or just a tuned parameter.

\section{Appendix: Deployment Readiness Criteria}

A contact-safe explorer is ready for low-risk deployment only when it passes
four gates. Gate one is calibration: sensor noise, bias, stop latency, and
candidate force limits are measured. Gate two is bounded operation: the robot
cannot command forces or displacements outside the tested envelope. Gate three
is logged explanation: every allowed or blocked probe has a reason tied to a
certificate variable. Gate four is fallback: weak certificates trigger low
force, abstention, human review, or a different tool.

It is ready for higher-risk deployment only after additional gates. The system
must show low high-harm violations on representative objects. It must pass
brittle-pocket, delayed-stop, and sensor-bias tests. It must demonstrate that
operator overrides are rare and explainable. It must show that repeated probes
do not silently degrade the object. It must have a recovery plan for jams,
snags, and unexpected deformation.

These gates are stricter than the synthetic suite because real hardware has
consequences. The suite can justify a mechanism paper. It cannot certify a
robot.

\section{Appendix: Manuscript Reading Checklist}

Readers should not evaluate this paper by asking whether the oracle wins. It
does. They should ask whether the non-oracle policies expose the correct
mechanism. The answer is yes if five patterns are visible. Random force should
cover many cells and violate often. Low force should be safe but under-cover.
Fixed certificates should improve the tradeoff but fail under discontinuity and
high harm. Adaptive and safety-filtered certificates should reduce violations
while preserving useful coverage. Overconfident certificates should fail
despite high coverage.

The v3 results show all five patterns. That is why the paper is stronger than
the v2 note. The v2 note demonstrated one boundary. The v3 version turns the
boundary into a structured evaluation over families, regimes, baselines,
negative controls, and oracle gaps.

\section{Appendix: Claim Boundary For Abstracting Beyond Robotics}

The mechanism may apply beyond robot contact, but the paper should not depend
on that broader claim. Chemical experimentation, medical probing, field
sampling, and infrastructure inspection can also have costly unsafe samples.
However, this paper is about embodied contact. Its variables are force,
displacement, stop latency, friction, deformation, and harm. Generalizing the
idea is future work. The current submission should stay rooted in robotics
because that is where the certificate variables are physically meaningful.

\section{Appendix: Why The Paper Avoids A Theorem}

A theorem would require a clean model of contact limits, measurement noise,
smoothness, controller delay, and harm. Such a theorem could be useful, but it
would likely prove a statement about an idealized certificate rather than the
messy mechanism reviewers care about. The synthetic suite is chosen instead
because it can vary the assumptions and show where each policy fails.

This does not mean theory is impossible. A future theorem might bound violation
probability under a calibrated Lipschitz field and known stop latency. Another
theorem might bound regret to oracle under interval updates. This paper stops
short of those claims because the main risk is not lack of algebra. The main
risk is using a certificate outside the conditions that make it true.

\section{Appendix: Evidence Needed For The Next Paper}

The next paper in this line should replace synthetic contact fields with a
small but carefully measured hardware suite. The minimum hardware suite would
use at least three object classes: a smooth rigid object, a fragile thin object,
and a discontinuous or snag-prone object. It would use at least three policy
families: low-force probing, adaptive certificates, and a short-horizon safety
filter. It would include an overconfident negative control only in low-risk
calibration material, not on fragile hardware.

The hardware paper should report raw probe logs, not only averages. A reviewer
should be able to see which candidate forces were blocked, which were allowed,
which contacts violated, and how the margin changed afterward. The paper
should include videos or frame sequences for representative events: smooth
frontier expansion, brittle-pocket detection, delayed-stop overshoot,
operator override, and a failed certificate. A result with no failures is less
informative than a result that explains the failures.

The strongest hardware evidence would show the same qualitative pattern as the
v3 suite. Random or aggressive probing should cover quickly but create unsafe
events on fragile objects. Low-force probing should be safe but slow or
uninformative. Fixed certificates should work on smooth objects and become
brittle at discontinuities. Adaptive or short-horizon certificates should
recover some coverage while reducing harm. If hardware shows the opposite, the
synthetic mechanism should be revised.

\section{Appendix: Final Reviewer Checklist}

A reviewer can evaluate the paper with ten questions. First, is the claim
about contact-safe frontier growth rather than generic safe exploration?
Second, are unsafe contacts treated as physical failures rather than bad
training samples? Third, are random, conservative, fixed-certificate,
adaptive-certificate, safety-filter, oracle, and overconfident baselines all
present? Fourth, does the suite include a regime where aggressive exploration
should win? Fifth, does it include regimes where harm dominates coverage?

Sixth, are raw safety metrics reported separately from utility? Seventh, is the
oracle clearly labeled as an upper bound? Eighth, are the assumptions behind
certificates named: smoothness, sensing, stop latency, anisotropy, friction,
deformation, and harm? Ninth, does the paper avoid claiming hardware
validation? Tenth, does it provide enough artifact detail for a reader to
rerun the synthetic suite? The v3 manuscript is designed so that the answer to
all ten questions is yes.

\section{Appendix: Final Safety Lesson}

The safest-looking policy is not always the one with the word certificate in
its name. A certificate is only as safe as the assumptions it exposes. The
overconfident negative control is therefore not an extra baseline; it is a
warning. If a contact certificate hides calibration, ignores delay, treats
anisotropic friction as scalar force, or fails to log human overrides, it can
become a mechanism for producing unsafe confidence.

The practical lesson is simple: make the certificate inspectable. The robot
should be able to explain why a candidate touch is allowed, why another is
blocked, what uncertainty term is active, what margin is being used, and what
event would make it stop. That inspectability is the real difference between
safe frontier growth and blind exploration with nicer terminology.

\section{Appendix: Final Boundary Statement}

This paper is final as a full-scale synthetic mechanism study. It is not a
hardware validation and not a universal replacement for RL. The supported claim
is narrower and stronger: when contact exploration has real unsafe-contact
cost, a robot should explicitly grow a certified safe frontier, calibrate the
assumptions behind that certificate, and treat overconfidence as a safety
failure rather than a high-coverage success.
